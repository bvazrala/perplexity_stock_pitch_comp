## 1. Why AI Data Center Connectivity Is the New Bottleneck

The GPU is no longer the sole constraint in building AI infrastructure. As hyperscalers push beyond 10,000-GPU clusters toward configurations approaching a million GPUs, every wire, fiber, and switch becomes a performance-limiting factor.

Three dynamics are converging to make this real:

**GPU density is exploding.** Rack-level GPU count has increased roughly 6x from 2021 to 2026 — from 8–16 GPUs per rack to 72+ in the GB200 NVL72. NVIDIA's Vera Rubin targets 576 GPUs per rack. As density scales, the bandwidth required to keep GPUs fed with data scales superlinearly. Every rack boundary crossed adds latency and consumes power.

**Copper is hitting its physical limits.** Traditional copper cables are impractical at 800 Gbps speeds across the server-to-switch distances common in modern AI clusters. Optical transceivers already consume 2–3x more power than copper at equivalent bandwidth — a critical constraint at 200+ kW per rack. Active electrical cables (AECs) from Credo represent a hybrid solution: they use copper but embed signal processing to extend reach while preserving power efficiency, currently winning at intra- and rack-to-rack distances up to 7 meters.

**Link reliability is existential.** AI training clusters operate as a single synchronized unit — a "link flap" (momentary connection dropout) on any cable can stall the entire cluster. Credo's internal data cited on its Q1 FY2026 earnings call found its AECs provide "1,000 times more reliable" connections than alternatives. NVIDIA's [$4B strategic investment](https://futurumgroup.com/insights/nvidias-4b-optics-bet-signals-photonics-as-ais-next-bottleneck/) in Coherent and Lumentum (March 2026) explicitly acknowledged that optical interconnect supply is now a gating factor on AI infrastructure deployment — confirmation from the highest authority in the industry that connectivity has become the bottleneck.

Credo sits directly at this intersection: its AECs dominate today's copper-phase deployments, while its optical DSP portfolio (ZeroFlap, Robin, Cardinal) is positioned to capture the ongoing optical ramp.

## 3. Why the Stock Dropped ~59% From Its Peak

The stock peaked at **$213.80** and fell to a trough near **$88** (a ~59% drawdown) before recovering to ~$101.45 — still **-52.5% from the high**.

Multiple factors layered on top of each other:

### 3a. Gross Margin Guidance Shocked the Market
Q3 FY2026 posted a 68.6% adjusted gross margin. Management guided Q4 to **64–66%**, a sequential compression of up to 460 basis points. The culprit: ramp-related costs from ZeroFlap optics and line card products, which carry lower initial margins. Investors who had priced in expanding margins were caught off guard. Rosenblatt cut its price target from $170 to $125 on the session.

### 3b. Customer Concentration Is Extreme
Three hyperscalers made up 39%, 32%, and 17% of Q3 revenue — roughly **88% of total revenue from just three customers**. Any demand pullback, a hyperscaler pivoting its interconnect strategy, or an inventory digestion cycle could cut deeply into a single quarter.

### 3c. The Stock Was Already Priced for Perfection
CRDO had run from its 52-week low of $29.09 to a high of $213.80 — a rally of >600% — during the prior twelve months. At peak, the stock was priced at roughly 40x forward sales. When margin guidance came in below expectations on an otherwise record quarter (revenue beat, EPS beat), high-multiple momentum stocks get repriced violently.

### 3d. Broader Macro Headwinds
The broader semiconductor and AI sector sold off in early 2026 alongside tariff concerns and risk-off sentiment. CRDO, as a high-beta AI infrastructure play, amplified general sector moves.

### 3e. Copper-to-Optical Transition Uncertainty
A structural concern: as hyperscalers eventually shift from AECs (copper-based) to co-packaged optics (optical), will Credo's AEC revenue erode? Bears argue the AEC business may be a temporary bridge rather than a durable franchise. New optical DSP products carrying lower initial margins reinforces this concern.

## 4. OFC 2026 Product Launches

Credo arrived at the Optical Fiber Communication Conference (OFC) in March 2026 with three major product announcements, marking its most significant optical push to date.

### ZeroFlap — 800G Optical Transceiver (Generally Available)
The [800G 2xDR4 ZeroFlap transceiver](https://www.stocktitan.net/news/CRDO/credo-launches-800g-zero-flap-optical-transceivers-engineered-for-ai-ul3s634c3mu6.html) directly attacks the link-flap problem that can halt AI training clusters. Built-in optics telemetry detects impending failures before they cascade, and automated fault response suppresses flaps that would otherwise stall GPU jobs worth millions in compute-time.

- Management pulled the production ramp forward by ~six months (from late FY2027 to Q1 FY2027)
- This product is the key catalyst for converting CRDO from a copper AEC company into an optical transceiver company
- Initial margins are dilutive but improve as volume scales

### Cardinal 1.6T Optical DSP (Sampling)
The [Cardinal family](https://credosemi.com/gated/cardinal-800/) is built on a **3nm process node** targeting **224G-per-lane** operation, supporting both 800G and full 1.6T links.

| Spec | Value |
|---|---|
| Process Node | 3nm |
| Lane Speed | 224 Gbps PAM4 |
| Power | <15W per 1.6T link |
| Latency | <40ns |
| Target Market | Next-gen AI fabric, supercomputing clusters |

### Robin 800G Optical DSP Family (Available)
The [Robin family](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/credo-introduces-robin-800g-optical-130000031.html) is Credo's **sixth-generation DSP architecture**, covering 800G and 400G applications across multimode and single-mode configurations.

| Variant | Speed | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Robin 800 | 800G | Multimode |
| Robin 802 | 800G | Single-mode (SiPh EML) |
| Robin 850 | 800G LRO | Multimode |
| Robin 852 | 800G LRO | Single-mode (SiPh EML) |
| Robin 400 | 400G | Multimode |
| Robin 402 | 400G | Single-mode (SiPh EML) |

> Lightwave Research projects 800G and 1.6T transceivers will exceed 120 million combined units in 2026–2027.

Collectively, ZeroFlap + Robin + Cardinal represent Credo's attempt to add a second growth engine alongside AECs. If both ramp simultaneously, the multiple revenue streams reduce customer concentration risk and expand total addressable market.
